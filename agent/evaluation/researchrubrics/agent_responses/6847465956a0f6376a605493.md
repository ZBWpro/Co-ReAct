Markov chains, named after Andrey Markov, are mathematical systems that undergo transitions from one state to another according to certain probabilistic rules. They are widely used in statistics and combinatorics to model systems that change states over time, where the next state depends only on the current state and not on the sequence of events that preceded it. This property, known as the Markov property, makes them particularly useful for analyzing processes with memoryless characteristics.

The genesis of Markov chains can be traced back to the early 20th century when Andrey Markov introduced them to study the properties of sequences of dependent events. His work was motivated by the analysis of the distribution of vowels in Alexander Pushkin's "Eugene Onegin," where he demonstrated that the probability of a vowel appearing could be modeled using a sequence of states. This foundational work laid the groundwork for the application of Markov chains in probability theory and statistics.

In contemporary research, Markov chains have found applications in various fields, including computer science, economics, and physics. They are particularly useful in modeling complex systems where the future state depends only on the current state. For instance, in the context of the "expected value of flips to get N heads in a row" problem, Markov chains provide a structured approach to calculate the expected number of trials needed to achieve a specific sequence of outcomes. This is achieved by defining states that represent the number of consecutive heads obtained so far and using transition probabilities to determine the expected number of flips required to reach the desired state.

The application of Markov chains to solve such problems involves setting up a system of equations based on the states and transitions. For example, to find the expected number of flips to get N heads in a row, one can define states $ S_0, S_1, \ldots, S_N $, where $ S_i $ represents the state of having $ i $ consecutive heads. The expected number of flips from state $ S_i $ to reach $ S_N $ can be calculated using the transition probabilities between these states. This method, known as first-step analysis, allows for the derivation of a system of linear equations that can be solved to find the expected value.

In summary, Markov chains are a powerful tool in statistics and combinatorics, providing a framework for modeling and analyzing systems with probabilistic transitions. Their genesis in the early 20th century and their continued relevance in contemporary research highlight their versatility and importance in various scientific disciplines. The application of Markov chains to problems like the expected number of flips to achieve a sequence of heads demonstrates their practical utility in solving complex probabilistic questions.

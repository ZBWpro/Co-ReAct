The question of whether courts can mandate memory erasure for violent offenders to prevent re-offense involves complex ethical and legal considerations. This policy brief argues that while memory erasure could potentially reduce recidivism, it should not be mandated by courts due to its profound implications for individual autonomy, moral responsibility, and the integrity of the legal system.

### Deontological Perspective
From a deontological standpoint, as emphasized in <cite id="72b4eb2d-1">AJ Kolber's 2006 paper</cite>, memory erasure violates the moral obligation to preserve personal identity and autonomy. Deontologists argue that individuals have a right to their memories, even if those memories are of harmful actions. Mandating memory erasure would constitute a violation of this right, as it removes the offender's capacity for self-reflection and moral accountability. This aligns with the ethical concerns raised in <cite id="72b4eb2d-5">the AMA Journal of Ethics</cite>, which highlights the potential for criminals to strategically manipulate memory to avoid consequences.

### Consequentialist Perspective
Consequentialism, as discussed in <cite id="72b4eb2d-3">J Yang's 2025 paper</cite>, suggests that the benefits of reducing recidivism must be weighed against the risks of erasing personal identity and creating a slippery slope toward authoritarian control. While memory erasure might lower the likelihood of re-offense, it could also lead to unintended consequences, such as the loss of effective deterrents and the potential for misuse by authorities. This is supported by <cite id="72b4eb2d-2">A Sharma's work</cite>, which notes that the fallibility of memory could undermine the effectiveness of current deterrent tactics.

### Historical Precedents
1. **The Use of Predictive Algorithms in Criminal Justice**: As highlighted in <cite id="72b4eb2d-4">the University of Virginia School of Law</cite>, the adoption of predictive algorithms in criminal justice has raised concerns about fairness and transparency. Similar issues could arise with memory erasure, where the criteria for selecting offenders for the procedure might be biased or opaque.
2. **False Memories in the Criminal Justice System**: <cite id="72b4eb2d-6">M Maples-Janisch's 2025 study</cite> demonstrates the risks of false memories, which could be exacerbated by memory erasure techniques. This raises concerns about the reliability of legal proceedings and the potential for wrongful convictions.
3. **Memory Manipulation and Legal Integrity**: <cite id="72b4eb2d-7">Dr. Julia Shaw's research</cite> shows that individuals can be convinced they committed crimes they did not, highlighting the dangers of manipulating memory. This underscores the need for strict safeguards to prevent abuse of memory erasure technology.

### Conclusion
While memory erasure may offer a promising tool for reducing recidivism, its use should be approached with caution. Courts should not mandate the procedure without robust ethical guidelines and legal safeguards to protect individual autonomy and the integrity of the justice system. The potential benefits must be balanced against the risks of moral and legal erosion, ensuring that the rights of individuals are not compromised in the pursuit of public safety.

Plautus' comedy *Pseudolus* offers a nuanced portrayal of Roman slavery that both reflects and subverts the legal and social realities of the Roman Republic. The play centers on Pseudolus, a clever and resourceful slave, whose actions challenge the master-slave hierarchy while also revealing the complexities of Roman slave society.

The legal framework of Roman slavery was rooted in the *status* of slaves as property, devoid of legal rights or autonomy. As noted in the analysis of Roman slavery, slaves were considered "rightless" and subject to the absolute authority of their masters (<cite id="0a8dea29-7">...</cite>). This legal system was reinforced by institutions such as the *familia*, where slaves were integral to the economic and domestic functions of the household. However, the play subverts this by depicting Pseudolus as a figure of agency and cunning, capable of manipulating the social order to achieve his goals.

Pseudolus' interactions with his master, Ballio, and other characters highlight the tension between the legal fiction of slave subordination and the reality of slave agency. For instance, Pseudolus pretends to be Ballio's slave to gain access to the household, yet his actions reveal a deep understanding of the social and legal structures that govern his status (<cite id="0a8dea29-6">...</cite>). This duality reflects the broader Roman context, where slaves, though legally property, could exert influence through wit and deception.

The play also critiques the Roman ideology of slavery by satirizing the master-slave relationship. Pseudolus' ability to outsmart his master and secure his freedom underscores the fluidity of social status in Roman society, challenging the notion that slaves were entirely powerless (<cite id="0a8dea29-8">...</cite>). Furthermore, the comedic elements of the play, such as Pseudolus' elaborate schemes, serve to highlight the absurdity of the master-slave dynamic, thereby subverting the dominant ideology.

In conclusion, *Pseudolus* provides a multifaceted view of Roman slavery, illustrating both the constraints imposed by the legal system and the potential for resistance and agency within it. The play's portrayal of Pseudolus as a cunning slave reflects the complexities of Roman slave society, where legal and social frameworks coexisted with the realities of individual agency and subversion.

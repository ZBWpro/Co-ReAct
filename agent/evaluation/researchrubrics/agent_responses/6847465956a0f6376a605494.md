The 1975 National Emergency in India, declared by Prime Minister Indira Gandhi, had profound and lasting impacts on the country's democratic institutions. Without this authoritarian episode, several critical developments in India's political landscape might have unfolded differently. 

In the short term, the absence of the Emergency would have preserved civil liberties and constitutional norms. The suspension of habeas corpus and judicial review during the Emergency allowed the government to bypass legal checks and balances. Without this period of authoritarianism, the judiciary might have maintained its independence, ensuring that the rule of law was upheld. The press, which was heavily censored during the Emergency, could have continued to function as a watchdog, holding the government accountable. The political opposition, which was suppressed during the Emergency, might have remained active, contributing to a more vibrant democratic discourse.

In the long term, the Emergency left a deep mark on Indian society and exposed the frail nature of its democracy. The experience of authoritarianism might have led to a stronger commitment to constitutional norms and civil liberties. The Emergency also had a significant impact on electoral politics, as the 1977 elections, which marked the end of the Emergency, were the first free and fair elections in India since 1952. Without the Emergency, the political landscape might have been more stable, with a more robust democratic culture. The Emergency also had a lasting impact on the Indian Constitution, as it led to the adoption of the 42nd Amendment in 1976, which sought to strengthen the role of the state in the economy and society. Without the Emergency, the Indian Constitution might have remained more focused on individual rights and freedoms.

In conclusion, the absence of the 1975 National Emergency would have likely resulted in a more stable and vibrant democracy in India. The judiciary, press, and political opposition would have continued to function as intended, preserving civil liberties and constitutional norms. The long-term effects on electoral politics and civil society would have been more positive, with a stronger commitment to democratic values and institutions.
